Polyphone puts your PolyAI voice agent directly on your website. Visitors click "Talk to our agent" and start speaking from the page they're already on – no number to dial, no app to install, no callback to wait for. You ship it from **Deployment > Widget** in Agent Studio.

![Polyphone launcher in the bottom-right of a website](/images/polyphone/hero-launcher.png)

> A fast way to bring PolyAI voice agents to your website – a simple voice entry point for customers, and a faster path to deploy, test, and scale digital voice for your team.

## Why Polyphone

Polyphone replaces a stack of awkward alternatives:

- **vs. a phone number on the page.** No PSTN minutes, no IVR menu, no waiting for the visitor to find their phone.
- **vs. a "request a callback" form.** No lag between intent and conversation. The agent picks up immediately.
- **vs. a chatbot.** Voice is faster and more natural for complex questions, accessibility, and hands-free use on mobile.
- **vs. internal Studio testing tools.** Calls land on the same production stack a real customer would hit – same gateway, same agent variant.

## Customer value

- **Faster deployment.** Paste a script tag, publish a variant, you're live.
- **More choice for customers.** Visitors who'd rather speak than type get a one-click route to your agent. Multilingual agents are surfaced in whichever languages they're configured for.
- **Easier testing and iteration.** Validate widget and agent behavior from a hosted preview before any code goes near your site.
- **Brand and site flexibility.** Spin up separate widget variants per site or per brand, with light or dark visual treatments.
- **A faster path for existing PolyAI customers.** Enterprise customers will be able to connect their existing voice agents to Polyphone for rapid website deployment once it's released to Enterprise.

## When end users reach for it

Polyphone is especially useful when:

- The customer can explain the issue faster by speaking than typing.
- The issue is urgent.
- The customer is on mobile, multitasking, or driving.
- Voice is more accessible than text for the user.
- The customer wants an immediate back-and-forth, not a queued reply.

## Best-fit launch use cases

Polyphone is strongest when deployed in targeted, high-value areas of your website where customers are already likely to need help:

- Support landing pages.
- Contact-us pages.
- Logged-in customer-service portals (claims, bookings, accounts, orders).
- Multilingual support journeys.
- High-intent conversion pages.
- Controlled pilots with known traffic volumes.
- Multi-brand sites that need separate voice experiences per brand.

> Start with a targeted page, validate engagement and call quality, then expand based on performance and demand.

## Polyphone in the channel mix

Polyphone is a voice channel, just like a phone number. The only thing that changes between them is how the audio gets to PolyAI.

| | Polyphone | Numbers (telephony) | Chat (webchat) | Voice (TTS settings) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| What customers do | Click a button on your site | Dial a phone number | Type in a widget on your site | – (configures how your agent sounds) |
| Modality | Voice | Voice | Text | Shared by all voice channels |
| Where you set it up | Deployment > Widget | Channels > Numbers | Deployment > Widget | Channels > Voice |
| Docs | This section | [Numbers](/telephony/introduction) | [Webchat](/webchat/introduction) | [Voice](/voice/introduction) |

Polyphone and Numbers are two ways into the same voice agent. They share the agent's spoken [voice](/voice/introduction) (set once, under **Channels > Voice**) and the same conversation logic. Switching a visitor between them doesn't require a separate flow.

## What you get

- **A configurable launcher.** Color, position, and label match your site, with light or dark visual wave treatments.
- **A consent step.** Visitors agree to mic access and your privacy notice before the call connects.
- **Mute / end-call controls.** Standard call UI built into the widget.
- **Basic call states.** Connecting, in call, ended, and error states are surfaced to the user.
- **Cross-tab safety.** Only one call can be active per visitor, even if they have multiple tabs open.
- **Domain allow-list.** The widget only loads on domains you've authorized.
- **Multiple widget variants.** Separate widgets per brand, region, or site, configured independently.
- **Multilingual voice agents.** Polyphone surfaces whichever languages the connected PolyAI agent is configured for.

## Compliance and consent

Polyphone includes optional controls for privacy disclaimer and consent. These are off by default and easy to switch on – see [Widget configuration](/widgets/configure).

## Where Polyphone fits today

Polyphone is purpose-built for fast deployment, controlled rollout, and website-based voice access. A few things to keep in mind as you plan your rollout:

- **It's a voice entry point, not a handoff layer.** Polyphone runs the call end-to-end on the same PolyAI agent. Voice-to-chat, chat-to-voice, and live-agent transfer aren't part of v1. If your launch journey needs a handoff today, pair Polyphone with [Numbers](/telephony/introduction) or [Webchat](/webchat/introduction) for that step.
- **It complements phone support, it doesn't replace it.** Use Polyphone alongside your existing phone channel to capture web visitors, while keeping your established hotlines for off-site customers.
- **It runs in the browser.** Mic permissions, network conditions, and mobile-browser behavior all matter. Test in your intended deployment environment before launch.

## Prerequisites

Before you create a Polyphone widget, you'll need:

- An Agent Studio project with a published variant.
- The website domain(s) where the widget will be embedded.
- Access to your website's HTML or your tag manager.
- An **HTTPS** site. Browsers require a secure context for microphone access.

You don't need a chat agent to use Polyphone – you can ship a voice-only widget. (Phone and Chat widgets share a single Studio surface, which is why both live under **Deployment > Widget** – pick **Phone** or **Chat** when you create one.)

## Technical details

<Accordion title="How a call connects">

```
Visitor's browser                           PolyAI
─────────────────────                       ──────────────
Polyphone iframe (mic, autoplay)
   │
   ├── HTTPS  ──────► Widget script endpoint  (loads widget JS)
   │
   └── WSS    ──────► WebRTC gateway          (signaling + Opus audio)
                            │
                            └─► your agent
```

Polyphone is loaded by a single `<script>` tag on your site. The script injects an iframe pinned to the bottom-right of the page. When a visitor clicks the launcher, the widget requests microphone access, opens a WebSocket to PolyAI's WebRTC gateway, and starts an Opus 48 kHz audio call with your agent.

Only one Polyphone call can be active per browser at a time. The widget enforces this with a cross-tab lock so a visitor with multiple tabs open cannot start parallel calls.

</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Polyphone vs. telephony, side by side">

| | Polyphone | Telephony |
|---|---|---|
| Transport | WebRTC over the browser | Phone network (PSTN / SIP) |
| Visitor sees | A widget on your website | A phone number to dial |
| Latency / quality | Opus 48 kHz over WebRTC | Carrier-dependent |
| Setup | Embed a script tag | Provision a number, configure SIP |
| Best for | Web-first journeys, support deflection | Inbound call centers, omnichannel routing |

You can run both: Polyphone for visitors already on your site, telephony for callers from anywhere else. Both connect to the same agent.

</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Programmatic control (JavaScript SDK)">

The widget script also exposes a `window.PolyphoneAPI` object that lets your page drive the call programmatically – useful when you want to trigger Polyphone from a custom button, an in-app event, or an existing chat experience.

```js
// Open the widget and start a call in one step.
window.PolyphoneAPI.connectAndCall();

// Pass context to the agent mid-call.
window.PolyphoneAPI.sendInfo("user_signed_in", { userId: "abc123" });

// React to lifecycle events.
window.PolyphoneAPI.onConnected(() => console.log("Call connected"));
window.PolyphoneAPI.onEnded(() => console.log("Call ended"));

// Remove a handler when you're done.
window.PolyphoneAPI.off("connected", myHandler);
```

Available commands: `connect`, `disconnect`, `startCall`, `endCall`, `connectAndCall`, `mute`, `unmute`, `sendInfo`, `isConnected`, `getCurrentSession`, `destroy`. Event registration uses dedicated methods: `onReady`, `onConnecting`, `onConnected`, `onDisconnected`, `onRegistered`, `onUnregistered`, `onRinging`, `onAnswered`, `onEnded`, `onFailed`, `onMuted`, `onUnmuted`, `onCaptureAudio`, `onAudioEnded`, `onInfoSent`, `onInfoReceived`, `onTranscription`, `onError`. Use `off(eventName, handler)` to remove a registered handler.

</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Browser support">

Polyphone uses standard WebRTC primitives. Verified browsers:

| Browser | Minimum version |
|---|---|
| Chrome / Chromium | 72 |
| Firefox | 60 |
| Safari (macOS) | 14.1 |
| Safari (iOS) | 16 |
| Edge | 79 |

Visitors on unsupported browsers see a disabled call button with a brief explanation. Mobile-browser and in-app-webview behavior can vary, so test in your intended deployment environment before launch.

</Accordion>
