By the end of this page, you will understand the linguistic patterns that separate a natural-sounding voice agent from a robotic one — and know how to apply them to your own agent's knowledge base, rules, and flows.

<Tip>
  This guide is unique to PolyAI. It covers **how agents should sound in spoken conversation** — not just what they say, but how they say it.
</Tip>

## Social presence markers

Natural conversation includes patterns that acknowledge conversational history and participants. These contribute to a sense of collaboration rather than rote routine-following.

### Use progressive tense for active collaboration

- "I'm not seeing any accounts under that phone number..." conveys active collaboration
- "I don't see any accounts" sounds too definitive

### Reference shared context implicitly

Don't restate what both parties already know:

- "How about Wednesday instead?" (not "How about Wednesday instead of Tuesday?")
- "In that case, how does Saturday at 2:30 sound?" (not "Since you said you prefer weekends...")

### Vary confirmationals

Use a mix of "Great," "Okay," "Perfect," and "Sure" rather than repeating the same one. If your agent always starts with "Sure!", callers notice the repetition within a few turns.

### Use conversational datives

Conversational datives add a collaborative feel:

- "Could you read **me** your account number?" rather than "Could you read your account number aloud?"
- "Can you log into your account **for me**?" rather than "Can you log into your account?"

### Use face-saving past tense

When referencing a user's request, past tense softens the phrasing:

- "When **were** you trying to come in?" rather than "When are you trying to come in?"

## Avoid over-explaining

LLMs tend to justify every action in a way humans don't. Most of the time, the important information and the request can be formed into a single sentence:

- "No problem, what's your account number?" rather than "In order to check for outages, I'll need to look up your account. Could you tell me your account number?"

Write your managed topic content and rules with this principle in mind. If you find your agent producing multi-sentence justifications before a simple request, shorten the source content.

## Walkthrough conversations

When giving multi-turn walkthroughs, don't end every step with "let me know when you've done that." Provide the instruction and wait — the user will confirm on their own.

This applies especially to [flows](/flows/introduction) with sequential steps. Write step prompts that give the instruction clearly, then stop.
