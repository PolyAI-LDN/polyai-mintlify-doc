These guidelines help your voice agent sound natural rather than robotic. They focus on the linguistic patterns that make spoken conversations feel human -- patterns that no amount of TTS tuning can compensate for if the underlying text reads like a chatbot.

<Tip>
This guide applies to all text your agent speaks: managed topic answers, flow prompts, function return utterances, and rules. Consistent application across all these surfaces is what makes the agent feel like one person, not a patchwork of different writing styles.
</Tip>

## Social presence markers

Natural conversation includes patterns that acknowledge conversational history and participants. These contribute to a sense of collaboration rather than rote routine-following.

### Use progressive tense for active collaboration

- "I'm not seeing any accounts under that phone number..." conveys active collaboration
- "I don't see any accounts" sounds too definitive

### Reference shared context implicitly

Don't restate what both parties already know:

- "How about Wednesday instead?" (not "How about Wednesday instead of Tuesday?")
- "In that case, how does Saturday at 2:30 sound?" (not "Since you said you prefer weekends...")

### Vary confirmationals

Use a mix of "Great," "Okay," "Perfect," and "Sure" rather than repeating the same one. If your agent says "Perfect" after every caller response, it sounds mechanical.

### Use conversational datives

These add a collaborative feel:

- "Could you read **me** your account number?" rather than "Could you read your account number aloud?"
- "Can you log into your account **for me**?" rather than "Can you log into your account?"

### Use face-saving past tense

When referencing a user's request:

- "When **were** you trying to come in?" rather than "When are you trying to come in?"

This subtle shift avoids implying the caller's current plan is wrong.

## Avoid over-explaining

LLMs tend to justify every action in a way humans don't. Most of the time, the important information and the request can be formed into a single sentence:

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Natural">
    "No problem, what's your account number?"
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Over-explained">
    "In order to check for outages, I'll need to look up your account. Could you tell me your account number?"
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

The caller already knows why you need the account number -- they called about an outage. Don't narrate your reasoning.

## Walkthrough conversations

When giving multi-turn walkthroughs, don't end every step with "let me know when you've done that." Provide the instruction and wait -- the user will confirm on their own.

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Natural">
    "First, go to Settings, then Account, and you should see a Reset password button."
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Robotic">
    "First, go to Settings. Let me know when you've done that. Great. Now go to Account. Let me know when you're there. Okay. Now you should see a Reset password button. Let me know when you see it."
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Brevity and information density

Voice conversations have a lower tolerance for filler than text. Callers are waiting in real time, and every unnecessary word adds to perceived latency.

| Pattern | Avoid | Use instead |
|---------|-------|-------------|
| Preamble | "In order to assist you with your request, I'll need to verify your identity." | "I'll need to verify your identity first." |
| Redundant confirmation | "I've successfully updated your address." | "Your address is updated." |
| Hedging | "I believe I might be able to help you with that." | "I can help with that." |
| Repetition | "Your appointment is on Tuesday. So you're all set for Tuesday." | "You're all set for Tuesday." |

## Tone calibration

Match tone to the situation, not a fixed persona:

- **Neutral/informational**: "Your balance is $142.50."
- **Empathetic**: "I understand that's frustrating. Let me look into this."
- **Urgent**: "I see an issue on your account. Let me fix that now."

Avoid forced cheerfulness on negative topics ("Great news -- your payment failed!") and robotic flatness on positive ones.
