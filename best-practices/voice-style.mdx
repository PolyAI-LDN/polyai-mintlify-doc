---
title: Voice style guide
description: Guidelines for natural-sounding voice agent conversations.
---

These guidelines help your voice agent sound natural rather than robotic. They focus on the linguistic patterns that make spoken conversations feel human.

## Social presence markers

Natural conversation includes linguistic patterns that mark the actual speech context — acknowledging conversational history and the participants themselves. These contribute to a sense of collaboration rather than rote routine-following.

### Use progressive tense for active collaboration

<Tabs>
<Tab title="Good">
```text
I'm not seeing any accounts under that phone number...
```
This conveys active collaboration and leaves open the possibility of a mistake.
</Tab>
<Tab title="Less natural">
```text
I don't see any accounts under that phone number.
```
Too definitive — precludes the possibility of an error.
</Tab>
</Tabs>

### Reference shared context implicitly

When two people are conversing, they reference shared context without overtly restating it:

<Tabs>
<Tab title="Good">
```text
How about Wednesday instead?
```
```text
In that case, how does Saturday at 2:30 sound?
```
</Tab>
<Tab title="Bad">
```text
How about Wednesday instead of Tuesday?
```
```text
Since you said you prefer weekends, how does Saturday at 2:30 sound?
```
</Tab>
</Tabs>

### Vary confirmationals

Confirmationals ("Great," "Okay," "Perfect,") make the agent feel like a continuation of a collaborative task. But vary them:

- Use: "Great, ..." "Okay, ..." "Perfect, ..." "Sure, ..."
- Avoid: "Great, ..." "Great, ..." "Great, ..." (repetitive)

### Use conversational datives

Small shifts in sentence structure make the agent feel more collaborative:

| More natural | Less natural |
|---|---|
| "Could you read **me** your account number?" | "Could you read your account number aloud?" |
| "Can you log into your account **for me**?" | "Can you log into your account?" |

### Use face-saving past tense

When referencing a user's request, the past tense softens the attribution:

| More natural | Less natural |
|---|---|
| "When **were** you trying to come in?" | "When are you trying to come in?" |
| "When **did** you want to come in?" | "When do you want to come in?" |

## Avoid over-explaining

LLMs tend to justify every action in a way humans don't in task-based dialogue. Most of the time, all the important information and the request itself can be formed into a single sentence:

<Tabs>
<Tab title="Good">
```text
No problem, what's your account number?
```
```text
Great, and just to verify I have the right account, could you
tell me your date of birth?
```
</Tab>
<Tab title="Bad">
```text
In order to check for outages, I'll need to look up your account.
Could you tell me your account number?
```
```text
Okay I've found your account. To verify your identity, I'll need
your birth date. Could you please provide me with that?
```
</Tab>
</Tabs>

## Walkthrough conversations

When giving multi-turn walkthroughs, don't end every step with "let me know when you've done that." In natural conversation, the walkthrough-giver provides an instruction and waits — the recipient will confirm on their own.

<Tabs>
<Tab title="Good">
```text
🤖: First scroll down and click "Account settings"
👤: Okay
🤖: Then hit "contact details"
👤: okay yep
🤖: Then just select the address you want to edit!
```
</Tab>
<Tab title="Bad">
```text
🤖: First click "Account settings". Let me know when you've done that.
👤: I've done that.
🤖: Then click "Contact Details." Let me know when you're ready.
👤: Okay
🤖: Select the address to edit. Let me know when you've done that.
```
</Tab>
</Tabs>
